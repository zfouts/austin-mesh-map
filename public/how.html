How the map is calculated

Every number on the map comes from a physical link budget over real terrain — here is the whole chain, with nothing hidden.

1. The link budget

For any path from a transmitter to a receiver, the predicted received power is:

RX power = TX power + TX antenna gain + RX antenna gain
         − path loss (terrain model, below)
         − clutter loss (environment, both ends)
         − fade margin

A point is "covered" when RX power clears the receiver's sensitivity (e.g. −126 dBm for Meshtastic LongFast). The difference is the margin shown throughout the app — positive margins are dB of headroom against real-world messiness.

2. Terrain

Elevation comes from the open AWS Terrain Tiles dataset (SRTM-derived, ~30 m; ~15 m USGS 3DEP data in the US at close zoom). Every path is sampled every few dozen meters, and terrain is dropped relative to the transmitter's tangent plane using the standard 4/3-effective-earth-radius convention, which is how atmospheric refraction and the radio horizon are conventionally modeled. Note SRTM partially includes tree canopy — a forested ridge reads slightly taller than its dirt.

3. Path loss — three engines

ITM (Longley-Rice v1.2.2) — used for point inspection, point-to-point links, and repeater candidate scoring. This is a JavaScript port of the NTIA reference implementation (public domain), the same algorithm behind SPLAT! and commercial planning tools. It analyzes the full terrain profile: horizon angles and distances, effective antenna heights, the Δh terrain-irregularity statistic, two-ray reflection in line-of-sight, blended knife-edge + smooth-earth diffraction beyond the horizon, troposcatter at extreme range, and climate-based statistical variability (predictions are the 50/50 median).

Delta-Bullington (P.1812-style) — selectable in the Model section. This is the diffraction construction at the heart of ITU-R P.1812, the model CloudRF defaults to for LoRa — meaning most MeshCore community coverage maps (e.g. the meshcore.co.uk map) are generated with this family. Our implementation covers the diffraction core (Bullington on the actual profile plus a spherical-earth correction via a smooth-profile Bullington) and omits P.1812's ducting/troposcatter/variability terms — hence "-style". For reference, the open-source Meshtastic Site Planner uses the same ITM engine we default to, so both community conventions are represented here.

Two-edge Deygout — used for coverage heatmaps, where ITM would be ~650,000× too slow. Free-space loss plus ITU-R P.526 knife-edge diffraction over the dominant terrain edge (picked by maximum elevation angle from the transmitter) and the strongest secondary edge beyond it. Faithful for one or two ridges; optimistic beyond three.

4. Clutter and fading

Terrain models don't know about buildings and trees, so a clutter loss is added at each antenna end. In the default Auto mode the value comes from NLCD land cover (30 m, US only) at that end's actual location — dense urban 20 dB, forest 15, low-density residential 12, grassland 3, open water 0, and so on — so a receiver in the Barton Creek greenbelt is penalized differently than one in a parking lot. Outside the US (or if the data is unavailable) it falls back to the manual environment setting: 0 (open), 6 (rural trees), 12 (suburban), or 18 dB (dense urban). Either way the loss fades linearly to zero as the antenna rises above the ~20 m clutter layer. A rooftop yagi at 10 m eats half the penalty; a tower eats none. The separate fade margin (default 6 dB) reserves headroom for multipath fading.

5. Antennas

The antenna list uses honest gains: where a marketing claim is physically impossible for the antenna's size (a 17 cm whip is a half-wave dipole, ~2.15 dBi, no matter what the listing says), the physics number is used and the claim is shown in the label. Measured VSWR from the Austin Mesh RF Index is converted to mismatch loss and subtracted — which is how a "3 dBi" antenna with VSWR 8.7 ends up modeled at −2.1 dBi. Directional antennas use a gaussian azimuth rolloff (G(θ) = G₀ − 12(θ/beamwidth)²) clamped at the front-to-back ratio.

6. Repeater placement

"Suggest repeater sites" is a maximin search: it lays a 42×42 grid (~1,700 candidates) over your sites' padded bounding box and scores every candidate by its worst link margin to any site — a repeater is only as good as its weakest leg. The grid pass screens with Deygout for speed; finalists are re-scored with ITM. Up to 15 spaced picks are shown, ranked best-first. Elevation is never an input — hilltops win only because height wins line-of-sight in several directions at once, and a well-placed saddle can and does beat a taller blocked summit.

8. Accuracy, honestly

The model chain was calibrated against observed links in the Austin MeshCore network (via scope reach data): it correctly classifies the large majority of real links, and its known misses are nodes on towers much taller than the assumed heights — antenna height is the biggest unknown, since the mesh data doesn't include it. Treat any single point prediction as roughly ±8–10 dB; the green/amber/red classifications and the ranking of repeater candidates are more trustworthy than any absolute number. It also knows nothing about land access — R1 may be in someone's back yard.
